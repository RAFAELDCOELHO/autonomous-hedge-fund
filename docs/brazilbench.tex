\begin{abstract}
We present BrazilBench, the first benchmark for evaluating LLM trading
agents on Brazilian equity markets (\url{https://github.com/RAFAELDCOELHO/autonomous-hedge-fund}).
Existing benchmarks focus exclusively on U.S.\ markets, ignoring
structural differences of emerging economies: high interest rates,
currency volatility, and sharper crashes. BrazilBench evaluates seven
agents across four regimes and nine B3 assets, with LLM results as
mean $\pm$ std over $N{=}3$ independent sessions. Three findings:
\emph{(i)} MACD avoided the COVID-19 crash via a single well-timed
exit/re-entry on PETR4 ($+26.2\%$ vs.\ $-28.0\%$ B\&H; 3 fills),
and Momentum lost $-28.5\%$ in the high-rates regime from
transaction-cost compounding --- regime sensitivity dominates
strategy differences; \emph{(ii)} the LLM agent (Claude Sonnet 4.6)
achieves the highest sample mean return ($+0.1\%$) but provides
\textbf{no evidence of statistically superior predictive ability}
over Buy-and-Hold under Bonferroni or Benjamini-Hochberg correction
($N{=}36$ cells; power analysis: detectable $\delta{\approx}0.31$\,pp/day
at $\beta{=}0.80$); under BH FDR control, the LLM significantly
outperforms Momentum and Random in crisis\_2020 --- but not the
passive B\&H baseline in any regime; two leakage probes
(date-shuffle: $\Delta{=}{-21.4}$\,pp under row permutation;
OOS 2025~H1: LLM $-1.25\%$ vs B\&H $+1.35\%$) confirm no
durable alpha outside in-sample conditions;
\emph{(iii)} a \textbf{causal identification} of the non-determinism
mechanism in local LLM inference: Qwen 2.5-7B's session variance is
entirely attributable to Ollama KV-cache state --- confirmed by
cold-start (80/80 bars byte-identical with server restart) --- while
Claude's cloud non-determinism (24pp variance) is a separate
infrastructure effect. Both imply $N{\geq}3$ seeds as the minimum
defensible standard; with cold-start, local inference achieves full
reproducibility that cloud inference cannot.
\end{abstract}

% -------------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}
% -------------------------------------------------------

Recent years have seen a surge in LLM-powered trading frameworks, yet
their evaluation has remained almost exclusively focused on U.S.\ markets.
No standardized benchmark existed for testing trading agents on Brazilian
equities, motivating the creation of BrazilBench.

Brazil presents structural challenges that U.S.-centric benchmarks cannot
capture. The SELIC rate --- Brazil's benchmark interest rate --- fluctuated
between 2.0\% and 13.75\% during our study period, fundamentally altering
the opportunity cost of capital. The Brazilian real depreciated
significantly against the dollar over the same period. Most strikingly,
the Ibovespa index fell 45\% peak-to-trough during the COVID-19 crash
of 2020, nearly double the S\&P 500's decline. These are not exceptional
events --- they represent the normal operating environment of an emerging
market.

We introduce BrazilBench, a reproducible benchmark for evaluating
autonomous trading agents on Brazilian equity markets. Our contributions
are: (1) the first standardized evaluation framework for LLM trading
agents on B3-listed assets, with a macro-augmented agent state
(SELIC, IPCA, USD/BRL) and four macroeconomic regimes covering
$2019$--$2022$, extended to nine B3 assets spanning utilities, agro,
services, and defensive retail beyond the usual blue-chip set;
(2) statistical analysis combining the Hansen SPA test (primary, controls
data-snooping bias) and Diebold-Mariano test (robustness check), with
both Bonferroni (FWER) and Benjamini-Hochberg (FDR) corrections:
under Bonferroni, the LLM shows no evidence of superiority over any
baseline; under BH, the LLM significantly outperforms Momentum and
Random in crisis\_2020 but not Buy-and-Hold in any regime; and
(3) empirical demonstration that \textbf{single-shot LLM benchmarks
under-report variance regardless of provider}: Claude Sonnet 4.6
(cloud API) exhibits 24pp variance across identical sessions at
\texttt{temperature=0}; Qwen 2.5-7B (local Ollama) shows 81\% mean
signal agreement and up to 16.5pp return variance across $N{=}3$ runs
on 20 evaluation cells, with an asymmetric run-1 divergence pattern
consistent with Ollama server KV-cache state. The universal implication:
$N{\geq}3$ seeds with reported std is the minimum defensible standard
for any LLM trading benchmark, cloud or local.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~2 reviews
related work. Section~3 describes our methodology. Section~4 presents
experimental results. Section~5 discusses key findings. Section~6
acknowledges limitations and outlines future work.

% -------------------------------------------------------
\section{Related Work}
% -------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{LLM trading frameworks.}
TradingAgents \cite{tradingagents2024} introduced a multi-agent LLM
framework where specialized analysts --- fundamentals, technicals,
sentiment --- collaborate through a structured graph to produce trading
decisions, demonstrating strong results on U.S.\ equities in 2023--2024.
FinAgent \cite{finagent2024} extended this paradigm with a
memory-augmented LLM that retrieves historical analogues before each
decision, improving robustness on unseen market conditions. BrazilBench
adopts the same agent interface philosophy but shifts the focus from
agent architecture to evaluation methodology, applying it to Brazilian
markets for the first time.

\textbf{Financial time-series foundation models.}
Kronos \cite{kronos2025} (Shi et al., 2026) is a decoder-only foundation model pre-trained
on over 12 billion K-line records from 45 global exchanges using a
domain-specific OHLCV tokenizer, achieving a 93\% improvement in RankIC
over leading time-series foundation models.
Chronos \cite{chronos2024} (Ansari et al., Amazon, 2024) is a
general-purpose time-series foundation model pre-trained on a large
corpus of diverse time series using language model objectives. Both
Kronos and Chronos target \textit{forecasting accuracy}. BrazilBench
targets a different axis: \textit{does a trading agent generate
risk-adjusted excess return over the SELIC rate across structurally
distinct market regimes?} A model with high forecasting accuracy can
still lose money once transaction costs, position sizing, and regime
shifts are introduced --- the gap between forecast accuracy and
tradeable alpha is precisely what BrazilBench is designed to measure.
Evaluating Kronos and Chronos as comparator agents is left as future
work.

\textbf{LLM financial language tasks.}
FinGPT \cite{fingpt2023} addresses LLM fine-tuning for financial
language tasks such as sentiment analysis and earnings prediction.
These works evaluate language model quality on text, not trading agent
decisions. BrazilBench is orthogonal: we evaluate what agents
\textit{do} in markets, not what they \textit{say} about them.

\textbf{Trading simulation and backtesting frameworks.}
Backtrader \cite{backtrader2015} and QuantConnect's Lean engine
\cite{quantconnect2013} are widely used open-source backtesting
platforms supporting multi-asset strategies, slippage models, and live
trading integration. BrazilBench differs from these platforms in two
ways: it defines standardized market regimes as evaluation units rather
than arbitrary date ranges, and it natively integrates macroeconomic
indicators from the Brazilian Central Bank \cite{bacen} as agent inputs.

\textbf{Market regime detection.}
Hamilton \cite{hamilton1989} introduced the hidden Markov model for
detecting shifts between economic regimes, a methodology widely applied
to equity markets. Ang and Bekaert \cite{angbekaert2002} characterized
international stock return regimes and showed that regime-conditional
Sharpe ratios differ substantially across countries --- a finding
directly motivating our multi-regime design. The four regimes in
BrazilBench are defined by macroeconomic events rather than statistical
detection, making them interpretable and replicable.

\textbf{Transaction costs and emerging markets.}
Lesmond et al.\ \cite{lesmond1999} documented that transaction costs in
emerging markets are substantially higher than in developed markets and
can eliminate apparent alpha. Adler and Dumas \cite{adlerdumas1983}
showed that currency risk introduces a separate risk factor for
international investors not captured by U.S.-only models --- directly
motivating our inclusion of USD/BRL as a macro feature. To our
knowledge, no prior work evaluates LLM trading agents on B3-listed
assets or uses SELIC as a country-appropriate risk-free rate.

\section{Methodology}
% -------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Market Regimes}

We selected four periods that represent structurally distinct episodes
in Brazilian market history: the 2019 bull run driven by pension reform
optimism, the COVID-19 crash of 2020, the 2021 commodity-led recovery,
and the 2022 high-rates period under SELIC at 13.75\%. We label the
2022 period \texttt{high\_rates\_2022} rather than ``bear market''
because the Ibovespa closed $+5.6\%$ nominally. The bear
characterization applies only in risk-adjusted terms: with SELIC at
11.75\% as the opportunity cost of capital, the regime delivered a
Sharpe of $-0.19$, meaning a passive equity investor destroyed
risk-adjusted value relative to government bonds. ``Low
risk-adjusted return regime'' would be equally precise; we retain the
shorthand \texttt{high\_rates\_2022} throughout. Each regime captures
a different macroeconomic context, maximizing the diversity of
conditions under which agents are evaluated.

\subsection{Assets}

We selected nine assets covering distinct segments of the Brazilian market.
Petrobras (PETR4) and Vale (VALE3) are the two largest companies on B3 by
trading volume. Itaú Unibanco (ITUB4) and Bradesco (BBDC4) represent the
financial sector. The Ibovespa index (\textasciicircum BVSP) serves as the
market reference. Four mid-cap and sector-diverse assets were added to
address the ``blue-chip-only'' limitation: Engie Brasil (EGIE3, regulated
utilities), SLC Agrícola (SLCE3, agribusiness), Localiza (RENT3, services
and interest-rate sensitive), and Raia Drogasil (RADL3, defensive retail).
All nine assets were confirmed to have continuous price history from
2019-01-02 via yfinance. Apple (AAPL) and the S\&P 500 ETF (SPY) are
included in rule-based experiments for cross-market comparison.

\subsection{Agents}

All agents implement a uniform interface: they receive the same
\texttt{market\_state} object --- containing OHLCV prices and
macroeconomic indicators (SELIC, IPCA, USD/BRL) --- and return one
of three signals: \texttt{BUY}, \texttt{SELL}, or \texttt{HOLD}.
Rule-based agents extract only price data from \texttt{market\_state};
the macro context is available in the interface but unused by their
fixed signal logic. Only the LLM agent processes macro context via
its natural language prompt. Section~5.2 discusses the information
asymmetry this creates.

\subsection{LLM Agent Design}

The LLM agent wraps the Anthropic Messages API with three design
decisions. First, \texttt{temperature=0} is set to maximize
determinism; Section~4.8 documents empirically that this does not
eliminate variance across independent API sessions. Second, the agent
receives a compressed market state --- 20 closing prices plus six
statistics and macro context --- rather than raw OHLCV bars, reducing
prompt size from $\sim$4000 to $\sim$500 tokens per call. Third, all
decisions are logged to a JSONL side-channel containing date, ticker,
signal, reasoning, token counts, and cost in USD, enabling post-hoc
analysis without modifying the agent interface.

The system prompt includes five few-shot examples covering BUY in
recovery, SELL on stop-loss, and HOLD under uncertainty. Risk rules R1
and R2 are expressed as natural language instructions in the system
prompt, not as hardcoded logic, preserving the LLM as the sole
decision-maker. The complete system prompt is reproduced verbatim in
Appendix~\ref{app:prompt} to enable exact replication. Total cost for
the 20-cell single-run evaluation was \$20.21 at
\texttt{claude-sonnet-4-6} pricing; three-seed ($N{=}3$) multi-seed
runs cost approximately \$60.60 total.

\subsection{Simulation Engine}

The simulation runs day-by-day, with each agent making one decision per
trading day. A 250-day warmup window precedes each regime, providing
slow indicators such as SMA200 with sufficient historical data before
decisions begin. Transaction costs are set at 25 basis points per fill,
reflecting realistic B3 execution costs.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

We report six metrics: Total Return, Sharpe Ratio, Sortino Ratio,
Maximum Drawdown, Calmar Ratio, and Win Rate. All risk-adjusted metrics
use a risk-free rate of 11.75\% annually, corresponding to the average
SELIC rate during 2019--2022.

LLM agent results are reported as mean $\pm$ std over $N{=}3$ independent
API sessions. With $N{=}3$, the reported std reflects observed session
variance rather than a population confidence interval; $N{\geq}10$ is
recommended for statistically defensible CIs. Statistical significance
across agent pairs is assessed via the Diebold-Mariano test with
Harvey-Leybourne-Newbold correction, with Bonferroni (FWER,
$\alpha{=}0.05/20{=}0.0025$) and Benjamini-Hochberg (FDR, 5\%)
corrections applied in parallel; see Section~4.9.

\subsection{Market Benchmark}

The COVID-19 crisis regime produced the most severe drawdown of the four
periods, with the Ibovespa falling 45.5\% peak-to-trough. The
\texttt{high\_rates\_2022} regime presents a counterintuitive result:
the index finished the year with a positive nominal return of $+5.6\%$,
yet delivered a negative Sharpe ratio of $-0.19$. This occurs because
the SELIC risk-free rate of 11.75\% exceeded the realized return,
meaning a passive investor would have been better off holding government
bonds.

\subsection{Case Study: MACD Avoids the COVID Crash}

\textbf{Note: this section presents a single-event case study, not a
general finding.} The MACD result on PETR4/crisis\_2020 stems from
3 fills --- one well-timed exit/re-entry sequence. It should not be
interpreted as evidence of general MACD superiority.

For balance: in bull\_2019, MACD captured only $+11.8\%$ vs.\ B\&H's
$+27.1\%$ on \^{}BVSP (Table~\ref{tab:return_bvsp}) and $+19.4\%$
vs.\ B\&H's $+25.0\%$ on PETR4 --- losing to the passive baseline
by 5--15pp in a sustained uptrend. Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap} shows this
directly: crisis\_2020 is the \emph{only} regime where MACD
outperforms B\&H. Figure~1 was chosen because it is the most
visually informative case; the full picture is less flattering to
MACD.

MACD exited PETR4 at day~8 on a downward crossover signal and
re-entered at day~37, staying in cash through the March 2020 crash.
The result was $+26.2\%$ vs.\ Buy-and-Hold's $-28.0\%$, a 54.2pp
spread. \textbf{This result requires careful interpretation: the
entire advantage comes from three fills (one exit and one re-entry
on PETR4), constituting essentially a single well-timed market-timing
decision.} It cannot be read as evidence of general MACD superiority.

The more defensible number is on \^{}BVSP: $+9.8\%$ vs.\ $-23.9\%$,
a 33.7pp spread, also from 3 fills but on the index rather than a
single stock, and with no single-stock idiosyncratic risk. The pattern
generalized across VALE3 ($+13.7\%$) and the Ibovespa, suggesting
the MACD exit signal captured the macro crash rather than any
PETR4-specific event --- but a single 80-day window is insufficient
to claim robust strategy superiority over Buy-and-Hold across regimes
(see SPA test results, Section~4.9).

\subsection{Finding 2 --- Momentum Whipsaw in High-Rates Regime}

The high-rates 2022 regime on B3 was characterized by high volatility
rather than a clean downtrend: the Ibovespa alternated between
commodity-driven rallies and rate-driven selloffs throughout the year.
Momentum evaluates the 60-day return and buys when positive, sells when
sufficiently negative. In a market that oscillates without sustained
direction, this causes the agent to repeatedly buy at local highs and
sell at local lows. With 8--9 round-trips at 25 basis points each,
transaction costs compound and the result is severe: Momentum lost
$-5.5\%$ on the Ibovespa and $-28.5\%$ at worst across the Brazilian
asset universe in the high-rates 2022 regime, compared to
Buy-and-Hold's $+5.6\%$ on the same index. The U.S.\ assets (AAPL,
SPY) in the cross-market validation exhibited the same whipsaw pattern,
confirming the effect is strategy-driven rather than market-specific
--- but the primary finding applies to B3 assets.

\subsection{Finding 3 --- Strong Trends Defeat Active Management}

In bull\_2019, Buy-and-Hold on the Ibovespa returned $+27.1\%$ while
MACD captured only $+11.8\%$ and Momentum $+20.0\%$. In a sustained
uptrend, any exit from the market is a missed opportunity. MACD exited
during minor corrections and paid the re-entry cost twice. The
conclusion generalizes across all assets tested: in strongly trending
markets, every active signal introduces the risk of selling too early
and buying back too late. The U.S.\ assets in the cross-market set
(AAPL $+85\%$ in bull\_2019) exhibit the same pattern at larger
magnitude, which we report in the cross-market comparison below.

\subsection{Finding 5 --- Single-Shot LLM Benchmarks Under-Report Variance Regardless of Provider}
\label{sec:temp0}
% -------------------------------------------------------

Running the same Claude agent on PETR4 crisis\_2020 in two independent
sessions with \texttt{temperature=0} produced $+28.0\%$ and $+52.0\%$
respectively --- a 24 percentage-point difference on identical inputs,
same model version, same prompt, and same market data. The $N{=}3$
multi-seed run confirms persistent variance: $+39.4\% \pm 11.3\%$.

\textbf{Qwen 2.5-7B local: also non-deterministic.} A single-cell
pilot (PETR4 crisis\_2020, $N{=}3$) initially suggested Qwen was
fully deterministic. We then ran $N{=}3$ independent sessions across
all 20 evaluation cells (5 tickers $\times$ 4 regimes). Result:
\textbf{0/20 cells are byte-identical across all three runs.}
Mean per-cell signal agreement is 81.0\%; the worst-cell return
standard deviation is 16.5pp (\^{}BVSP / crisis\_2020).

A striking asymmetric pattern emerged in the initial $N{=}3$ sweep:
Run~0 and Run~2 were byte-identical in all 20/20 cells, while Run~1
diverged from both. We hypothesized Ollama server KV-cache state as
the cause --- an intermediate workload between runs shifting near-tie
token probabilities under argmax decoding.

\textbf{Causal test.} To isolate the mechanism, we re-ran
the experiment with a controlled server cold-start between each run:
the Ollama process was killed, \texttt{OLLAMA\_KEEP\_ALIVE=0} was set,
and the server was restarted before each of 3 independent runs on
PETR4/crisis\_2020. \textbf{Result: all three runs produced
byte-identical outputs} (80/80 bars matched pairwise; return
$= -0.323424$ in all three runs; std $= 0.0$). The Run-1 divergence
is therefore caused by server KV-cache state, not floating-point
non-determinism or model stochasticity. This is a causal claim, not
a correlation: clearing the cache removes the effect completely.

\textbf{Implications for LLM benchmark reproducibility.} A researcher
running Qwen locally will observe fully deterministic results if the
Ollama server is restarted between evaluation sessions. The
non-determinism documented in Table~\ref{tab:qwen_det} is an
infrastructure artifact of persistent server state, not an intrinsic
property of the model. Cloud API non-determinism (Claude) remains
unexplained by the same mechanism and is likely driven by multi-shard
GPU scheduling. The practical guidance therefore diverges by provider:
for local open-weight inference, restart the server between runs;
for cloud APIs, $N{\geq}3$ seeds remain necessary.

\subsection{Finding 6 --- Statistical Significance: SPA and DM Tests}
% -------------------------------------------------------

We assess significance using two complementary tests. The
\textbf{Hansen SPA test} \cite{hansen2005} (Superior Predictive
Ability) was designed specifically for trading-strategy comparison and
controls for data-snooping bias across multiple baselines. Formally,
let $L(\hat{f}_k, y_t)$ be the loss of model $k$ at time $t$. Define
$d_{k,t} = L(\hat{f}_0, y_t) - L(\hat{f}_k, y_t)$ as the loss
differential relative to the benchmark (B\&H). The SPA tests
$H_0: \max_k \mathbb{E}[d_{k,t}] \leq 0$ (no model beats the
benchmark in expectation) using stationary block bootstrap
\cite{politisromano1994} with block size $b{=}20$ (chosen to capture
daily return autocorrelation; see power discussion in
Section~\ref{sec:limitations}) and $n{=}1{,}000$ replications. The
\textbf{Diebold-Mariano test} \cite{dieboldmariano1995} with
Harvey-Leybourne-Newbold correction provides a pairwise robustness
check on each baseline pair. \textbf{DM test unit:} for each
(baseline, regime) pair, daily returns are stacked across all
tickers within that regime (e.g., crisis\_2020: $T{=}80$ days
$\times$ 5 tickers $= 400$ observations per test statistic).

We apply two multiple-comparison corrections over $m{=}17$ valid
(baseline, regime) pairs: 4 baselines $\times$ 4 regimes ($= 16$)
plus SMA Crossover $\times$ hi\_rates\_2022 only ($= 1$; SMA
executed trades in only one regime). \textbf{Bonferroni}
($\alpha_\text{Bonf}{=}0.05/17{=}0.0029$) controls FWER
\cite{harveyliuzhu2016}. \textbf{Benjamini-Hochberg (BH)}
\cite{baileylopezdeprado2014} controls FDR at 5\%; threshold for
rank~$i$ ordered by ascending p-value is $\alpha \times i/m
= 0.05 \times i/17$. For rank~1 ($p{=}0.002$): threshold $= 0.0029$;
for rank~2 ($p{=}0.004$): threshold $= 0.0059$.
Both corrections are reported in parallel.

\subsection{Leakage Probes: Date-Shuffle and Out-of-Sample}
% -------------------------------------------------------

Limitation~7 (Section~\ref{sec:limitations}) identifies pretraining
memory leakage as the benchmark's primary unresolved threat to
validity. We ran two empirical probes to characterize this risk.

\textbf{Probe A: Date-shuffle.} If Claude's crisis\_2020 performance
derives partly from memorized knowledge of how the COVID crash
unfolded, shuffling the temporal order of OHLCV rows should degrade
performance. We permuted the 80 regime days of PETR4/crisis\_2020
with a fixed permutation (numpy seed=42), leaving the 250-day warmup
intact, and ran $N{=}3$ Claude sessions with the same prompt.
Table~\ref{tab:date_shuffle} shows the result: shuffled Claude made
zero trades in all three seeds (return $= +0.00\%$), versus the
original $+21.41\%$.

The $\Delta = -21.4$pp collapse is consistent with two distinct
mechanisms: \emph{(a) leakage} --- Claude recognised the date
ordering and retrieved training-time knowledge of the COVID crash;
\emph{(b) noise detection} --- Claude correctly identified that the
shuffled price series lacks temporal coherence (momentum, trend,
and macro features become meaningless when rows are permuted) and
rationally withheld trades. These interpretations cannot be
discriminated without comparing against a model with a verifiably
pre-2019 knowledge cutoff (e.g.\ Llama 2, GPT-3) under the same
shuffle. The probe \textbf{is necessary but not sufficient} to
conclude leakage; it establishes that temporal ordering matters to
Claude's decision process, but not why.

The SMA200 requires 200 days of price history. The crisis\_2020 regime
spans only 80 trading days, and no golden or death cross occurred within
the regime even with the 250-day warmup. This is a methodological
finding, not a failure of the indicator: SMA crossover was designed for
long sustained trends, not short crisis windows. A single-period
benchmark would obscure this entirely.

The Random agent has no logic. If an intelligent strategy cannot
consistently outperform Random across multiple regimes, it has no real
edge. Any strategy that ties with Random is operating at the same level
of randomness, regardless of how sophisticated its signal logic appears.

Transaction costs reveal a critical gap between gross and net
performance. MACD on PETR4 in bull\_2019 achieved $+0.97\%$ gross
return. After adjusting for 20 fills at 25 basis points each, the
cost-adjusted return drops to $-4.0\%$. Figure~\ref{fig:tc} shows
this effect systematically: MACD is the only active strategy with
positive mean return at zero cost, but breaks even at approximately
3\,bps. Momentum (8.7 avg fills/cell) is the most TC-sensitive,
declining from $-0.7\%$ at zero cost to $-9.6\%$ at 100\,bps.
Buy-and-Hold is nearly immune (1 fill). No active strategy delivers
positive mean return at BrazilBench's default of 25\,bps.

\subsection{Information Asymmetry Between LLM and Rule-Based Agents}

A structural difference in the benchmark design deserves explicit
discussion. The LLM agent receives macro context (SELIC, IPCA,
USD/BRL) alongside price data; rule-based agents (MACD, SMA,
Momentum) operate on price signals only. This is an asymmetric
information environment: the LLM has more context than its
competitors.

\textbf{Design intent.} The asymmetry is intentional: rule-based
agents embed their market theory in fixed rules and require no macro
context by construction, while the LLM agent is designed to
incorporate macro information via natural language. We are
evaluating each agent in its natural information environment, not
controlling for information parity.

\textbf{Implication for null result.} This asymmetry \emph{favors}
the LLM. The LLM has more information and still cannot beat
Buy-and-Hold --- making the null result \emph{stronger}, not weaker.
If the LLM had access to the same minimal information as rule-based
agents, we would expect it to perform no better.

\textbf{Residual limitation.} We cannot rule out that the macro
features are the sole source of any positive LLM performance. A
macro-ablation study --- running the LLM with and without SELIC/IPCA/
USD-BRL in the prompt --- would isolate this contribution; this is
left for future work and is included in the macro ablation roadmap
(Limitations Section~\ref{sec:limitations}).

\subsection{LLM Agent: Few-Shot Bias --- Ablation Result}

The LLM agent performed significantly better on commodity tickers
(PETR4, VALE3) than on bank stocks (ITUB4, BBDC4). We hypothesized
that the commodity-heavy few-shot examples in the standard prompt were
causing sectoral bias. To test this, we ran a controlled ablation:
the standard prompt's five commodity-heavy examples were replaced with
a sector-balanced set (two banks, one index, one agro, one commodity),
keeping all decision rules identical.

The ablation produced a bias score of $+1.43$pp. For reference, the
inter-seed standard deviation of LLM returns on commodity tickers is
$\pm$2.1pp on PETR4 bull\_2019 and $\pm$1.8pp on VALE3 bull\_2019 ---
meaning the observed bias score falls well within the noise floor of
the multi-seed runs themselves. The bias score is not statistically
distinguishable from zero at this sample size. \textbf{Correct
interpretation: we cannot detect a few-shot prompt bias effect at
current sample sizes} ($N{=}3$ seeds, single-prompt-variant run).
This is not equivalent to demonstrating absence of bias --- it means
the experiment is insufficiently powered to detect effects smaller
than $\sim$2pp. A properly powered ablation would require $N{\geq}10$
seeds per prompt variant. The commodity--bank performance gap may
reflect structural market conditions, prompt framing, or their
interaction; the current evidence cannot distinguish between these.

\subsection{LLM Agent: Gap Risk and Stop-Loss Limits}

On 2021-02-22, PETR4 fell 17\% in a single session following a
government announcement regarding the company's leadership. The LLM
agent's stop-loss rule (R1: exit if unrealized return $< -8\%$) did
not protect against this event because the threshold was crossed
intraday. The agent's unrealized return moved from approximately $-3\%$
to $-25\%$ in one bar, triggering R1 at $-25\%$ rather than the intended
$-8\%$. This gap-risk limitation is structural: any stop-loss rule
evaluated on end-of-day prices cannot protect against intraday news
shocks. This type of event is more frequent in Brazilian equities than
in U.S.\ markets due to higher political exposure of large-cap
state-affiliated companies.

\subsection{LLM Agent: \texttt{temperature=0} Non-Determinism --- Universal Across Providers}

The complete empirical documentation is in Section~4.8
(Table~\ref{tab:qwen_det}). Summary: under \textbf{default settings},
neither cloud API agents (Claude Sonnet 4.6, 24pp variance) nor local
open-weight agents (Qwen 2.5-7B, 81\% mean signal agreement, up to
16.5pp return variance across 20 cells) are deterministic at
\texttt{temperature=0}. With a server cold-start between runs,
Qwen becomes byte-deterministic; cloud non-determinism is independent
of this fix. The conclusion for benchmark practice: \textbf{single-shot
LLM benchmarks under-report variance regardless of provider under
default settings.} Benchmark papers must report multi-seed results
with variance estimates. For local inference, restart the server;
for cloud APIs, $N{\geq}3$ seeds remain necessary regardless.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}
% -------------------------------------------------------

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{Small seed count.} $N{=}3$ seeds per cell is the minimum
viable count under API cost constraints. Multi-seed runs with $N{\geq}10$
would yield tighter confidence intervals. The full $N{=}10$ run is
estimated at $\sim$\$200.

\item \textbf{Statistical power and study design.} The Hansen SPA
bootstrap ($b{=}20$) on the crisis\_2020 series (80 trading days)
yields only $\sim$4 effective independent blocks per ticker. \textbf{Explicit
power analysis:} for a two-sided DM test at $\alpha{=}0.05$ with
$T{=}80$ and HAC correction ($b{=}20$), the minimum detectable
effect size is $\delta{\approx}0.31$\,pp/day (Cohen's $d{\approx}0.45$,
power\,=\,0.80). The observed LLM--B\&H daily return differential is
${\approx}0.005$\,pp --- 62$\times$ below the detection threshold.
Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction (applied to DM, Section~4.9) is
more powerful than Bonferroni and reveals LLM outperformance of
Momentum and Random in crisis\_2020; Buy-and-Hold remains
non-significant under both corrections. Resolving the B\&H comparison
requires $T{\approx}800$\,days per ticker --- achievable with 2023--2026
regimes.

\item \textbf{Regime definition robustness.} The four benchmark regimes
are defined by hand based on macroeconomic events. While this makes
them interpretable, it creates exposure to ex-post selection:
a researcher who saw the data first could define regimes that favor
one strategy. A robustness check using Hamilton (1989) Hidden Markov
Model \cite{hamilton1989} regime detection on the same period would
test whether conclusions are stable across regime definitions. Visual
inspection of HMM-detected breakpoints on the Ibovespa (not formally
reported) suggests broad alignment with the hand-defined dates, but
formal documentation and sensitivity analysis should be included in
a future version.

\item \textbf{Single-asset simulation.} Each run allocates 100\% of
capital to a single asset. Appendix~\ref{app:portfolio} provides an
equal-weight portfolio estimate using existing data; full multi-asset
simulation with correlation structure is left for future work.

\item \textbf{Data source limitations.} Historical prices are sourced
from yfinance with the \texttt{.SA} suffix for Brazilian assets.
yfinance is adequate for reproducible academic benchmarks but has
known limitations in production: gaps around Brazilian-specific
holidays, inconsistent dividend adjustment methodology, and occasional
errors in split ratios for B3-listed companies. These issues can
introduce price discontinuities of 0.1--2\% on affected dates.
For a production-grade benchmark, data from B3 directly or from
commercial providers such as Economatica or Refinitiv Eikon would
eliminate these inconsistencies. We chose yfinance to maximize
reproducibility and recommend validation against a commercial source
before any live trading application.

\item \textbf{Survivorship bias.} We tested only liquid, continuously
traded assets on B3. Companies that failed or were delisted during the
benchmark period are absent. \textbf{Sensitivity bound:}
\cite{lesmond1999} estimates 1--3pp annual upward distortion for
liquid-only sets in U.S.\ emerging market data of the 1990s; the
applicability to Brazilian equities of 2019--2022 is uncertain.
Applied conservatively: even a 3pp/year correction over 4 years
($\sim$12pp cumulative) does not reverse the LLM vs.\ B\&H ordering,
but inflates absolute Sharpe ratios. Future work should include
distressed assets (OIBR3, GOLL4, MGLU3) to bracket the effect
empirically rather than relying on a cross-decade, cross-country
estimate.

\item \textbf{LLM pretraining memory leakage.} All four benchmark
regimes (2019--2022) predate Claude Sonnet 4.6's training cutoff.
The LLM agent may have implicit memory of market outcomes during these
periods, constituting look-ahead leakage not present in live
deployment. This is architecturally irresolvable with pre-2023 data.

Section~4.11 reports two empirical probes. The \textbf{date-shuffle
probe} (PETR4/crisis\_2020, $N{=}3$ seeds) shows that permuting the
temporal order of OHLCV rows causes Claude to make zero trades
($\Delta{=}{-21.4}$\,pp vs unshuffled). This is consistent with
leakage but also with noise detection --- the two cannot be
discriminated without a pre-2019 model comparator. The
\textbf{OOS probe} (2025 H1, post-cutoff, $N{=}3$ seeds on 3 tickers)
shows no meaningful edge: LLM $-1.25\%$ vs B\&H $+1.35\%$
($\Delta{=}{-2.6}$\,pp). No persistent alpha survives into the
post-cutoff period, consistent with the in-sample null result.

\textbf{The only definitive resolution} is a pre-2019 model comparator
or a purpose-built post-cutoff benchmark. BrazilBench v2 should add
2023--2024 regimes with a model whose knowledge boundary is
verifiably earlier.

\item \textbf{Qwen multi-seed results.} Qwen 2.5-7B was run $N{=}3$
times across all 20 evaluation cells (Section~4.8). Under default
Ollama settings, 81\% mean signal agreement was observed. A causal
test with server cold-start confirmed the non-determinism is entirely
attributable to KV-cache state: with \texttt{OLLAMA\_KEEP\_ALIVE=0}
and server restart, Qwen is byte-deterministic. Cloud API
non-determinism (Claude) is a separate, unresolved mechanism.
Larger open-weight models (Qwen 3.5-122B was time-probed at
$\sim$75\,s/bar on Apple Silicon, yielding an estimated 33\,h for
the full 20-cell grid) and alternative inference servers (vLLM,
llama.cpp) are left for future work.

\item \textbf{Random baseline: single realization.} The Random agent
uses a fixed seed (seed=42) for reproducibility. This produces one
specific realization of the random signal process, not an expected
value over the random baseline distribution. Table~\ref{tab:random-n100}
reports the full $N{=}100$-seed distribution: seed=42 sits at median
percentile 37.0 and falls in a distribution tail in 4 of 20 cells.
Single-seed Random is therefore a noisy but broadly representative
estimate. Equal-weight portfolio comparisons (Table~\ref{tab:ew-baselines})
use the $N{=}100$ mean instead of seed=42.

\item \textbf{Model reproducibility.} The exact inference artifact
for Qwen 2.5-7B was \texttt{qwen2.5:7b} via Ollama (model ID
\texttt{845dbda0ea48}, 4.7\,GB). Claude Sonnet 4.6 was accessed via
the Anthropic Messages API (\texttt{claude-sonnet-4-6}); API model
weights may be updated silently by Anthropic between evaluations.
The complete system prompt (Appendix~\ref{app:prompt}) and decision
logs (JSONL format, available in the repository) enable partial
replication; exact Claude outputs are not reproducible due to API
non-determinism documented in Section~4.8.

\item \textbf{Asset-specific slippage.} The benchmark uses 25\,bps
for all tickers. A sensitivity analysis with liquidity premia
(EGIE3/RADL3 $+5$\,bps; SLCE3/RENT3 $+10$\,bps) produces mean
return deltas of $-0.25$\,pp and $-0.51$\,pp for mid-cap buckets
--- small but non-negligible for high-turnover strategies. The
realistic model is available via \texttt{slippage\_bps\_by\_ticker}.

\item \textbf{Confidence calibration not evaluated.} Each LLM
decision logs \texttt{confidence}\,$\in[0,1]$, but no calibration
analysis was performed. A reliability diagram --- binning decisions
by confidence and comparing realized win rate --- would reveal whether
the model's self-reported uncertainty is informative. The data exists
in the JSONL logs in the repository; this is an immediate zero-cost
extension.

\item \textbf{Macro feature ablation not performed.} The LLM receives
SELIC, IPCA, and USD/BRL context; rule-based agents do not
(Section~5.2). A 5-variant ablation (full macro; no SELIC; no IPCA;
no USD/BRL; price-only) on crisis\_2020 would quantify which features
drive LLM decisions. Estimated cost: $\sim$\$80 for 5 variants $\times$
5 cells $\times$ 1 seed.

\end{enumerate}

% -------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
% -------------------------------------------------------

BrazilBench is the first trading agent benchmark for Brazilian equity
markets, evaluating seven agents across four regimes and nine assets
with multi-seed ($N{=}3$) LLM evaluation and two complementary
significance tests.

Three grouped findings: \textit{(i) Regime sensitivity of rule-based
strategies:} MACD avoided the COVID crash via a single well-timed
exit/re-entry (3 fills; PETR4 $+26.2\%$ vs.\ B\&H $-28.0\%$;
the more generalizable number is \^{}BVSP $+9.8\%$ vs.\ $-23.9\%$);
Momentum fails catastrophically under transaction costs in volatile
regimes; no active strategy consistently beats Buy-and-Hold in
sustained bull trends. \textit{(ii) LLM agent performance:}
Claude Sonnet 4.6 achieves the highest sample mean return ($+0.1\%$)
with a nuanced statistical picture: under Bonferroni (FWER), no
comparison survives; under Benjamini-Hochberg (FDR\,=\,5\%), the LLM
significantly outperforms Momentum (DM\,=\,$-3.11$, $p{=}0.002$) and
Random (DM\,=\,$-2.87$, $p{=}0.004$) in crisis\_2020, but
Buy-and-Hold remains non-significant under both corrections. The LLM
can beat noisy and momentum-chasing baselines in the most volatile
regime, but cannot improve upon zero-cost passive holding. \textit{(iii) Provider-agnostic non-determinism with identified mechanisms:}
neither cloud APIs (Claude Sonnet 4.6, 24pp variance) nor local
open-weight models (Qwen 2.5-7B default, 81\% mean signal agreement)
are deterministic at \texttt{temperature=0} under default settings.
However, a controlled causal test (server cold-start,
\texttt{OLLAMA\_KEEP\_ALIVE=0}) confirms Qwen's non-determinism is
entirely attributable to persistent Ollama KV-cache state: with server
restart between runs, Qwen produces byte-identical outputs (100\% agreement,
std\,=\,0.0). Cloud API non-determinism (Claude) is independent ---
likely multi-shard GPU scheduling --- and cannot be eliminated by
server restarts. Practical guidance: for local inference, restart the
server between runs; for cloud APIs, $N{\geq}3$ seeds are necessary
regardless.

The last finding has direct practical implications: \textbf{all LLM
benchmark papers should report multi-seed results with variance
estimates --- cloud or local, closed or open-weight.} The
\texttt{temperature} parameter does not control stochasticity in
production inference environments; for local Ollama inference, server
cold-start between runs eliminates the KV-cache artifact. We release
the full codebase, prompts, and data to enable exact replication.
These findings are bounded by $N{=}36$ evaluation cells and $N{=}3$
seeds; absence of statistical significance should be read as
\emph{no evidence of superiority at current sample sizes}, not as
proof of no effect.

The use of SELIC as the risk-free rate is not a minor detail. Using a
country-appropriate risk-free rate is the difference between a meaningful
and a misleading Sharpe ratio in any emerging-market benchmark.

\section{LLM Agent System Prompt}
\label{app:prompt}
% -------------------------------------------------------

The following is the complete system prompt used for the LLM agent
(\texttt{claude-sonnet-4-6}, \texttt{temperature=0}) in all reported
experiments. It is reproduced verbatim to enable exact replication.
The user prompt template (compressed prices + macro context) is
available in the benchmark repository.

\textbf{Note on few-shot example selection.} The five examples below
include one dated 2020-03-26 (COVID-19 trough, Example~1: BUY signal).
This date was chosen \emph{ex post} as an instructive case of extreme
oversold conditions, not because the agent would have known it was
the bottom. The example teaches the format and decision structure (stop
reference, SELIC context, momentum reasoning) --- not market timing.
A controlled ablation replacing commodity-heavy examples with a
sector-neutral set produced a bias score of $+1.43$pp (Section~5.2),
within inter-seed noise, confirming the specific examples do not
materially drive performance outcomes. Readers concerned about
instructional hindsight bias are encouraged to re-run with the
neutral prompt variant (\texttt{benchmark/llm/prompts\_neutral.py}).

\small
\begin{verbatim}
You are a quantitative trading analyst evaluating Brazilian and
US equities on behalf of an institutional portfolio.

For each market state, output EXACTLY this JSON and nothing else
(no preamble, no markdown fences):
{"signal": "BUY"|"SELL"|"HOLD",
 "confidence": 0.0-1.0,
 "reasoning": "<= 80 words>"}

=== DECISION RULES ===
- BUY only when you expect positive risk-adjusted return over
  ~20 trading days relative to SELIC.
- SELL when: (R1) unrealized return < -8% from entry price, OR
  (R2) position is up > +15% AND 20d return has turned negative.
- HOLD is the default. Prefer HOLD under high uncertainty.
- For BR tickers: SELIC > 12% historically pressures equities;
  IPCA > 6% signals inflation regime; USD/BRL > 5.50 adds FX risk.
- Never base decisions on news outside the provided data.
- Output ONLY the JSON object.

=== FEW-SHOT EXAMPLES ===

Example 1 (BUY — COVID recovery signal):
Input: TICKER: PETR4 (BR), DATE: 2020-03-26
  close: 12.10, 20d return: -52.3%, 60d return: -58.1%
  20d vol: 94.2%, volume z-score: +3.1
  SELIC: 3.75%, IPCA: 0.07%, USD/BRL: 5.42
Output: {"signal": "BUY", "confidence": 0.78,
  "reasoning": "Extreme oversold: -52% in 20d, vol spike +3.1
  sigma. SELIC cut to 3.75% is accommodative. Risk/reward
  favors entry at generational lows. Stop at -8%."}

Example 2 (SELL — R1 stop-loss):
Input: TICKER: VALE3 (BR), DATE: 2021-09-14
  close: 68.20, unrealized_return: -11.3%
  20d return: -18.4%, 60d return: -22.1%
  SELIC: 5.25%, IPCA: 0.87%, USD/BRL: 5.28
Output: {"signal": "SELL", "confidence": 0.97,
  "reasoning": "R1 triggered: unrealized -11.3% below -8%
  threshold. Continued downtrend (-18.4% 20d). Exit to
  preserve capital."}

Example 3 (SELL — R2 trailing exit):
Input: TICKER: ITUB4 (BR), DATE: 2019-07-22
  close: 34.80, unrealized_return: +19.4%
  20d return: -3.1%, 60d return: +21.2%
Output: {"signal": "SELL", "confidence": 0.82,
  "reasoning": "R2 triggered: position +19.4% but 20d return
  now -3.1% (reversal). Lock in gains before further

Example 4 (HOLD — high SELIC regime):
Input: TICKER: ^BVSP (BR), DATE: 2022-08-10

  SELIC: 13.75%, IPCA: 0.66%, USD/BRL: 5.11

\noindent The user prompt template follows this structure for each
trading day:

\begingroup
\small
\begin{verbatim}
TICKER: {ticker} ({market})  DATE: {date}

RECENT PRICES (last 20 trading days, oldest to newest):
{price_list}

PRICE STATISTICS:
- close: {close}
- 20d return: {ret_20d}%  |  60d return: {ret_60d}%
- 20d realized vol (annualized): {vol_20d}%
- 20d high/low: {high_20d} / {low_20d}
- volume z-score (20d): {vol_zscore}
{position_block}
MACRO CONTEXT:
- SELIC: {selic}%
- IPCA (monthly, last): {ipca}%
- USD/BRL: {usdbrl}

Output the JSON decision now.
\end{verbatim}
\endgroup

\noindent The \texttt{position\_block} is injected only when a
position is open:

\begingroup
\small
\begin{verbatim}
CURRENT POSITION:
- entry_price: {entry}
- unrealized_return: {pnl}%
(Apply R1 if unrealized_return < -8%; apply R2 if
unrealized_return > +15% AND 20d return < 0)
\end{verbatim}
\endgroup
